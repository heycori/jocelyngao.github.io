ATLANTIS TRANSIT HUB


TEAM: JOCELYN GAO, BEN STONE, GRIFFIN TORNHEIM
CONCEPTS: PERSONALS, MODELING STATES OF DATA, TYPOGRAPHY, GRIDS, MOTION
SKILLS: DATA ANALYSIS, PERSONA/STAKEHOLDER ANALYSIS, VISUAL DESIGN, PITCHING
TOOLS: FIGMA, AFTEREFFECTS

Large data displays in transportation stations are where the majority of travelers get their information from, but they are often confusing and draining due to the massive amounts of information they display at once. Traveling is already stressful and taxing, and the last thing tired travelers want is to spend energy just to get frustrated trying to find their travel information.

Our goal for this project was to design a data-driven display that satisfied both user and stakeholder needs by allowing travelers to easily obtain the information they need while promoting the PTF Pass, which was a stakeholder's program that offered travelers better prices, more flexibility, and more personalization of travelers' trips.

​ We achieved our goal through our visual design of the display and through incorporating other tools, such as motion and an interactive kiosk control that enabled us to put some control back into the travelers' hands.

DESIGN PROCESS


DATA ANALYSIS



CURRENT STATE


FUTURE STATE



​ We wanted to fully understand all of the data we had on the file we received (click here to see) by first determining categories that information fell into for each transportation method (plane, train, and ferry). Then, we used those categories to model the current existing state we were in.

​ Next, we looked the data we had and determined what data to filter out by thinking about the bare minimum amount of information that travelers would need access to. Within this data pool, we created a model to communicate the data we would include as well as its information hierarchy. Within each transportation method of plane, train, or ferry, the hierarchy is as follows:
1. Destination
2. Departure time
3. Departure gate/platform/dock
4. Status
5. Whether that trip is PTF compatible


PERSONA AND STAKEHOLDER NEEDS



​ To continue optimizing the display design for our users, we identified our users' and stakeholders' needs by analyzing the personas for this project through identifying desire words in their descriptions. Identifying all user needs also allowed us to develop a concrete meaning to the initially vague PTF Pass Program that appealed to all wants and needs.

Persona 1: Elizabeth Duarte
- Stakeholder persona
- Created the PTF Program
- Wants travelers to be a part of her new program
- Wants to educate travelers about the PTF Program
- Offers travelers great value and flexibility
- Can combine the different modes of transportation





Persona 2: George Shimko
- User persona
- San Juan Island Resident
- Wants his children and grandchildren to visit him
- Plans to gift his children PTF passes to encourage them to visit​
- Wants his family to explore the San Juan Islands
- Use his property as a base





Persona 3: Patrick Mulvaney
- User persona
- Frequent traveler to the San Juan Islands
- Wants flexibility in case of npredictable weather changing travel times
- Wants to utilize all three transportation modes
- Wants to purchase multiple trips






PERSONAS TO PTF PASS





Taking the needs identified into consideration, we were able to define the PTF Program in a way that satisfied all of the personas' needs:
- Stakeholder: wants to promote the PTF Program for wide adoption so the usage of transportation methods is more balanced, and the Atlantis Hub has more loyal customers
- Users: want flexibility to accommodate for last minute changes, low costs so they are able to travel and explore more of the San Juan Islands, and more personalization of how they will get from one location to another


INITIAL ITERATIONS



After creating an information model and defining the PTF Program, we starting ideating in low-fidelity potential ways we would want to display the information, and landed on a horizontal display that incorporated a map of the San Juan Islands with all the possible routes to the islands.






ITERATIONS: DIGITAL PROTOTYPE








After our first 2 digital iterations of the horizontal display, we realized that either the data displayed was overwhelming and crammed, or the content didn't fit the horizontal display well. This prompted us to experiment with designing for a vertical display instead, and saw that we were able to display the data in a much cleaner and digestible way.


STYLE GUIDE








ADDING A CONTROL TO CREATE VALUE








After ideating many solutions with hand sketches, our team decided that the best control to implement was the interactive kiosk that would offer all types of users ease and privacy. It offers ease in that users would be able to purchase the PTF Pass and/or tickets right then and there at the kiosk, without having to wait in a long line to make travel arrangements through a desk attendant, and it offers privacy in that no one else can see what users are doing on the screen.



On the kiosk, users can click on their destination of interest to see all possible routes, and the arrival times, departure times, and price of each route. Then they can purchase the ticket that best suites their needs (and the PTF Pass if they are not already a member).

Then users will have to scan their PTF Pass on the scanner to the left of the screen, and can collect their tickets in one of two ways: if they scanned their PTF Pass with their mobile card, they can download their ticket onto their phone, or they can click “PRINT” for a physical copy.


ADDING MOTION TO CREATE VALUE



Reflecting back on our findings in our initial digital iterations of having too much information on the screen at once when displaying information for all three transportation methods, we decided to use motion to avoid this. The top half of the display rotates between three screens, where each screen only displays the travel information for one transportation method at a time. The middle portion displaying the map remains stable to minimize the amount of movement on the screen that would be distracting to users.

Normally a banner ad promoting the PTF Pass will be located at the bottom of the large display. However, once users click on their destination of interest on the kiosk, it will activate the large display to replace the PTF Pass banner ad with a banner that highlights all of the fun tourist attractions one can do at the location that the kiosk user clicked on.

This is meant to give users of the kiosk the ability to “explore the islands” prior to actually exploring them, and is meant to generate interest in the San Juan Islands from passersby.


On the kiosk control, motion is used to map out all possible routes to the user's destination of interest so that they can see all the different combinations of transportation modes they can take to get to said destination as well as the overall direction of travel. Additionally, motion is used to instruct those with the PTF pass to scan their pass on the reader next to the screen.

ATM CONTROL REDESIGN


CONCEPTS: PERCEPTUAL AFFORDANCE, FEED FORWARD, FEEDBACK, UNIFORMITY, STATE, EXPRESSION, COUPLING, DIRECT/SEMANTIC CARRIERS OF MEANING
SKILLS: 3D PROTOTYPING, CONTROL ANALYSIS, SKETCHING
TOOLS: EXACTO KNIFE, FOAM CORE, MARKERS

We encounter tens-if not-hundreds of controls everyday, yet most are designed poorly, making it frustrating or simply awkward when controls don't meet our expectations. How many times have people tried to push open a door that could only be opened by pulling? How many buttons exist that don't indicate its function?

​ In this project, my goal was to improve an existing ATM's control through adding and/or altering any combination of its indicators, such as perceptual affordance, carriers of meaning, feed forward, feedback, uniformity, zoning, etc., to provide users with a better meaningful experience that serves their needs.

​ The two things that people want when they use ATMs are safety and convenience. To address to these needs, my final redesign consists of an overall smaller control so users can use their bodies to conceal the actions they take on the ATM, a finger scanner as an alternative form of identification verification, rezoning of the current controls by actions users will take, and reshaping of existing buttons to clearly communicate and differentiate their functions. I have always been interested in design, calligraphy, and understanding how others think, but I fully discovered my passion for HCI in 2017 after taking courses in psychology, computer coding, and human robot interaction, and joining an HCI research team dedicated to mitigating the negative impacts of racism and microaggressions.

DESIGN PROCESS


RESEARCH





EXPERIENTIAL EXPLORATION
​ Having never used an ATM before, my first step was to find an ATM and to physically experience it. Positives of the design included:
- Overall, there were great semantic carriers of meaning to indicate how a user should interact with certain parts of the ATM
- Buttons stuck out of the control panel to clearly indicate perceptual affordance
- Coupling of "beeps" with pressed buttons was great feedback
- Uniformity of certain buttons clearly indicated relationships between those buttons' functions
On the other hand, the ATM was so wide that throughout the entire cash-withdrawal process, I had to continuously step left and right to use the control and to interact with the screen. Additionally, to retrieve my cash, I needed to awkwardly bend backwards to see the compartment and to get the cash.

INFORMAL ACADEMIC SEARCH
​ Despite the findings from my ATM experience, I am not the user. So I went online to read blogs and articles about others' experiences with ATMs to discover additional pain points and opportunities. What I discovered was that the biggest concern users had with ATMs was security. Financial information and money is extremely important, and more and more people are either getting robbed or having their information stolen from them.

​ INTERVIEWS
​ Next, I conducted guerilla research by standing near the ATM I was focused on redesigning, and asked those that used the ATM questions about their experience to find more insights and pain points. These questions revealed many users' worries and frustrations of forgetting their PIN numbers. People choose to use ATMs over waiting in line at a secure bank for the speed and convenience that ATMs provide, so users get extremely frustrated when they forget their PIN numbers and need to spend additional time to figure out and/or reset their PIN code.

​ USER NEEDS
​ All of the research revealed two key ATM user needs:​ safety and convenience.



ITERATION 1






To address the user needs for safety and convenience, I brainstormed many alternative methods of verifying one's identification, and narrowed it down to a finger scanner since it is minimally invasive, eliminates the potential for lurkers to see one's PIN code, and is already used as a form of ID verification for other scenarios, showing that people are comfortable with this level of technology.

​ Next, I sketched different forms the finger scanner could take, as well as the different icons that could be used to communicate its function to users, and created a 3D prototype of the optimal design pair to present to peers in an in-class critique. Feedback revealed that although people would use a finger scanner to verify their ID over entering their PIN codes, they would still want the option of entering their PIN number.

ITERATION 2



Previous Next



Taking the feedback I received from the critique, I kept the PIN number as an option for verifying one's ID, but also wanted to further address the user need for safety through rearranging the essential components of the current ATM to fit a smaller control panel.

​ I decided to organize the components in the order that users would use them in a left-right flow. The first column would be input actions; the second column would be identification verification actions; and the third would be confirmation/take-away actions. Due to the large quantity and wide variety of areas the user could interact with the control, LED lights would glow green in areas that needed user input as a call-to-action.

​ Additionally, I redesigned the shape of the confirm, clear, and cancel buttons to reduce the chances of users selecting the wrong button to ensure a smoother, quicker process. On the top of the buttons, I used hot glue to create braille beads to accommodate for the visually impaired.​

​ After another critique, I received feedback that left-to-right may not be the best process for ATM users. Some components are used in different stages of the process, such as the card reader where users would need to insert their cards at the beginning of the process, and retrieve it at the end. With the current left-right organizational pattern, users would need to travel all the way back to the left of the panel to retrieve their card, risking the chance of forgetting to take their cards.

Other feedback indicated that the confirm, clear, and cancel buttons should be reordered in a more commonly used order to further reduce the potential of selecting the wrong button.


FINAL ITERATION





I incorporated the feedback I received into the last iteration, and reorganized the different user actions in a top-down and left-right manner, and rearranged the cancel, clear, and confirm buttons to follow a more commonly used order. Same as the second iteration, areas that need user input would glow green to attract the user's attention.

​ After a long process of researching, brainstorming, and iterating, it was time to communicate the thought process and value of my redesign through a pitch to stakeholders. My pitch highlighted the two key user needs of safety and convenience through a story of Lisa, a single mother of two young boys who just won a huge soccer game and were promised by their mom to get dinner at their favorite cash-only diner. I chose feature a single mother in my story to emphasize the need for safety, and made her the mother of two young boys who just won a soccer game and are starving to reiterate the importance ATMs providing users ease, convenience, and safety.

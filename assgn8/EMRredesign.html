EMR REDESIGN


DESIGN CHALLENGE: 1 WEEK DURATION


SKILLS: DATA VISUALIZATIONS, VISUAL DESIGN, UI DESIGN, INFORMAL INTERVIEWING
TOOLS: FIGMA

Hundreds of patients come through a medical office each day, and attached to each patient is an EMR that contains their personal and medical information. Currently many EMR systems display the patient's data in an impersonal and boring way, which is problematic. As the day progresses, nurses and physicians are getting tired, making it more and more difficult to look at loads of data that is not visually appealing.

That is why it was my goal to redesign the EMR intake form and data visualizations to have a more fun layout that also humanizes the data.

DESIGN PROCESS


RESEARCH



INFORMAL INFORMATIONAL SEARCH

â€‹ Coming into this design challenge, I had no knowledge on what an EMR was, its purpose, how it looked, etc. In order to get familiar with EMRs, their current layouts, the type of information they held, and their purpose, I went online to read articles, watch videos, and view images of EMRs. Next, I conducted three semi-structured phone interviews with those in the medical field to find insights, pain points, and opportunities in how to improve the EMR strictly through design.

EMR Function:
- EMR stands for electronic medical record
- Information held:
- Patient profile information
- Reason for today's visit
- Past health (immunizations, surgeries, etc.)
- Current health (allergies, medications, etc.)
- Family history
- Lifestyle (tobacco and alcohol usage)
- Nurse/assistant inputs information
- Physician has little time to quickly glance over information



Opportunities:
- Separate information by category
- Visually design the EMR to be more visually aesthetic
- Simplify options
- Visually represent data in a humane way
- Use meaningful color code to make the EMR more vibrant


IDEATION: HAND SKETCHES









â€‹ INFORMATION CATEGORIZATION AND ORGANIZATION

â€‹ The first design decision I needed to make was how to group the information EMRs held in a way that made sense to both the patient and nurse.

The nurse needs to know who the patient is and their basic medical information in order to give a more personalized and positive visit experience to the patient, which is why the patient profile is the first portion of the intake form. Next, the nurse will address the patient's main concern: the reason for today's visit. The logic is that once the patient's main need is satisfied, they will be more happy and willing to provide additional information to the nurse. Then, following the trend of current conditions, the nurse will collect the patient's current health information, including allergies, medications, and other conditions.

â€‹ After current patient information is collected, the nurse will transition to collecting past health information, which includes everything from vaccinations to surgeries to conditions. With the theme of past medical information, the nurse will proceed to collect family history information.

The final section of the intake form is information on the patient's alcohol and tobacco usage. Even though this falls in with the "current" information category, I chose to make this the last section of the intake form because after interacting with the nurse for a longer period of time, the patient will be more comfortable and more likely to answer their alcohol and tobacco usage honestly.

DESIGN IDEAS

Once the order of information collected was determined, I began sketching ways to collect the information.








Then, I sketched ways that the physician could visualize the data in the EMR form.





In addition to being able to view all the information as text in the box layout, I wanted to provide doctors/physicians with the option of viewing the same information in more comprehensive forms. My first idea (left image) was enabling physicians to view current and past health information on a silhouette where circles/ellipses indicated the location of previous conditions and where physicians could hover over/click to learn more.

â€‹ The second idea I had was displaying the family's medical history in a tree format that allows the physician to trace conditions the patient has through the branches of the tree, which in the final iteration would also incorporate a color to each condition to facilitate tracking the patient's conditions.


FINAL DESIGN DECISIONS






Box Layout
After exploring multiple possible layouts the EMR could take, the one that made the most sense was a layout organized by boxes. This is because by combining boxes with colors, it clearly communicates what information is in the same category, and keeps the information well organized.





Color and Font
Throughout the entire EMR, whether it's for the intake form or through viewing the patient's medical history, the boxes will follow the color code on the left. I chose to use colors to indicate what type of information is shown as a way for users to quickly identify information type as well as incorporate some "fun" into the EMR.

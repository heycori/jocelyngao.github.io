THE LOOKING GLASS


INCREASING ENGAGEMENT WITH PUBLIC ART


TEAM: JOCELYN GAO, TIMMY CHIU, NORMAN KANG, BIDISHA ROY
CONCEPTS: OBSERVE AND INTERCEPT, REVERSE ASSUMPTIONS, AFFINITY DIAGRAMS, STORYBOARDS, SPEED DATING, EXPERIENCE PROTOTYPING
SKILLS: CUSTOMER JOURNEY AND STAKEHOLDER MAPS, DATA VISUALIZATIONS, INTERVIEWING TECHNIQUES, POSTER AND ELEVATOR PITCH
TOOLS: FIGMA, TABLEAU, MICROSOFT EXCEL

Public art serves many purposes: it's visually pleasing; it acts as a landmark; it serves as a statement that the city cares about its art and culture; and it tells a story about the city and its community. Yet because public art is extremely expensive to install and even more costly to maintain, questions about its value remain. Is public art "worth" it? Does it improve the livability of a city?

Our team conducted a lot of research and interviews, and analyzed and synthesized the data to find two key insights that would drive our solution:
1. There's both value in engaging with and observing others engage with public art
2. People are looking for conversation pieces that create interactions and connections between strangers​
Based on these insights, we re-framed our problem and asked ourselves: How can we use public art to help citizens express their creativity and connect to the city?

This led us to our solution, The Looking Glass: a transparent panel situated between the user and the public art piece where the user can use dry-erase markers to add to the image of the art without actually physically altering the art. Passersby can view the contributions of others and alter, add to, and/or replace it. Through testing our prototype, we saw an increase in citizen engagement with public art and others nearby.

However, just our team seeing the positive results from our experience prototyping session wasn't enough. We needed to communicate the value we created with public art to others through a poster and elevator pitch. In less than two minutes, our team summarized the most important findings from the past semester that drove us to our solution, as well as what our solution was and what it offered.



DESIGN PROCESS


BACKGROUND RESEARCH



BEFORE THE TEAM

​ Before we were assigned teams, we individually conducted background research to understand the space and current state of public art, and analyzed previous interviews and research data to create a customer journey map, a stakeholder map, and data visualizations.












RESEARCH GOALS AND SCOPE

Once we were assigned teams, we discussed our previous findings and insights with a "Walk the Wall" activity, and generated new insights from all of our data.





Then to find our project direction and focus, we did a re-framing activity to push our thinking out of our comfort zones. The re-framing activity helped us find our project focus of how we might allow public art to change post-installation, or be dynamic.





Given our scope, we defined our research goals and research plan that we would follow for the project.





OBSERVE AND INTERCEPT

With our research plan and goals determined, our team wrote scripts for observe and intercept interviews for two different types of interviewees: those that engaged with the public art and those that passed by it. We wanted to understand why some people would take the time to interact with the art, whereas others would barely acknowledge it. Each team member interviewed one person in downtown Pittsburgh, for a total of 4 interviews. Right after, we went into an interpretation session, and affinity diagrammed our findings and insights.








The key insights we synthesized were:
- People are more likely to revisit art if the revisit is of high value (“worth it”)
- People are more intrigued by unexpected and unnatural elements (eyeball chairs vs fountain)
- Landscaping around art can affect perception of art itself (ex: yellow leaves framing green statue)
- Interactive art increases value in the form of contribution, game element, emotional, visual and experiential value
- Interactive art that allows public input (eg. locks on bridges) offers value by allowing contributors to feel part of something greater than themselves; community / belonging
-Public art may be considered less prestigious than museum art/galleries


NEW PROJECT DIRECTION

Initially, our focus was primarily on how to incorporate dynamic art as a means of increasing the value of public art post-installation, but our findings showed that the average Pittsburgh citizen did not value public art more if there was a dynamic component to it. To redefine our scope, we did another "Walk the Wall" activity, generated insights, identified patterns across interviewees, and found that Pittsburgh citizens wanted to connect with their community through public art, and wanted a conversation piece like public art so they can have conversations with strangers nearby. We then re-framed our question to be: "How might we increase community involvement in public art to facilitate social connections?"





STORYBOARDS AND SPEED-DATING

From affinity diagramming, we also identified user needs and individually created Crazy 8's of design ideas that satisfied those needs. Then, as a team, we voted on our favorite ideas that we would then turn into storyboards for a speed-dating session.






Overall findings and insights from the speed dating session were that since people are respectful of public art, they are unlikely to physically alter or modify public art in any way without first receiving explicit permission or encouragement from the city of Pittsburgh and/or the artist. Upon receiving permission and guidance in how to contribute to/alter the art, people are most likely to engage if the activity is low commitment and low effort.


PROTOTYPE TESTING



​ FIRST ITERATION

Our first prototype was one transparent 6"x10" lid that would be placed in between the user and the public art piece. Users could contribute to the screen with dry erase markers to indirectly modify the image of the public art. An eraser would also be available to each screen to see how willing people were in erasing others' contributions. Also, to ensure that citizens knew that they could draw on the board, there was signage nearby encouraging them to draw.

We wanted our solution/experience/prototype to be accessible for the highest amount of people as reasonably possible: no technological barriers; minimal effort; and intuitive. We also chose markers because it is a lower barrier of entry for people, and invites people that have little time and drawing ability to engage with the prototype. Additionally, markers are a cleaner medium to use, which would encourage people to contribute, since our research revealed that users would not interact with messy mediums, such as paint.







​Given the positive results from experience prototyping in class, we kept the majority of the components from iteration 1 in iteration 2, such as the transparent screen, the dry erase markers, and the signage. Changes made were that the transparent screen was much larger and was also sitting on stands for more stability. With a new prototype, we wrote an experience prototyping plan with a script, questions, and target participants to interview, and went out into the field to test.









DOCUMENTATION OF EXECUTION OF METHOD

​The weather was sub-optimal in that it was 38 (felt like 30) degrees and windy, which created an environment where people did not want to stand outside and use their hands to draw. Furthermore, due to the wind, a few members of the team had to stand by the setup to make sure nothing fell. This changed the dynamic of the experiment as it seemed people were more hesitant to approach a crowd of people huddling around a stand. However, our presence may have also increased the intrigue surrounding the prototype. Users drew a wide range of objects on the prototype and used a variety of colors.


REFLECTION OF THE METHOD

​Positives:
- Users found that it was a “fun” and novel experience
- The board served as a boundary object that helped facilitate interactions with strangers and public art
- The “less polished” prototype was inviting to passersby


Negatives:
- Users were unsure of what to draw on the prototype
- The prototype was too unstable to stand on its own and required members to hold it up
- Cold weather resulted in low foot traffic


KEY INSIGHTS

- Active engagement and value can be found in two actions:
- Interacting in the form of drawing
- Interacting in the form of actively observing others draw
- People are looking for conversation pieces that create interactions and connections between strangers​

POSTER PITCH



​ To summarize our semester-long's work and findings, we designed a poster and that highlighted the problem, methods used, and key insights that drove us to our solution for how to make public art more valuable to the community. We then pitched our poster to a diverse audience, including Carnegie Mellon students, faculty, and stakeholders of the public art project.
